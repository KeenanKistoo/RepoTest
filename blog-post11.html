Reflection on Travelling While Trans - Sasha Costanza-Chock

This article discusses the author’s experience when going through the airport wave scanning machine. A particular reference in the article that highlights the severity of the lack of inclusive technology would be when the author mentions how sure that they will be “uncomfortable, embarrassed, humiliated” (Constanza-Chock, 2020: 3). An individual who is not breaking any rules (such as carrying drugs, illegal weapons, etc.) should not feel uncomfortable when approaching these sort of mandatory checks.

There are several ways to avoid a situation like this which are also discussed in different parts of The Design Justice Network (Constanza-Chock, 2020: 6). However, I do think that to some extent it boils down to educating individuals about issues that these marginalised groups face in their daily lives. By educating individuals consistently, they will be able to approach the situation more respectfully. Yes, several designs and procedures are not inclusive enough but, it will be counterproductive to only improve how technology and algorithms are designed without educating the individuals who control/oversee the use of this technology.

Finally, marginalised groups should be in control or constantly oversee improvements to the technology as the author discusses (Constanza-Chock, 2020: 6). I, as an Indian male, have the ability to program certain things like games and websites, however, I do not bear the same experiences as individuals like the author; therefore, I cannot effectively program something that will directly influence certain groups without a discussion and constant feedback. Naturally, the solution would be effective communication and constant improvements to not only include some marginalised groups but all.
