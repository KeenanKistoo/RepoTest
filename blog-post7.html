Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information:

In this reading, we are made aware of the access, or lack of access, certain countries have to the internet. The statistics illustrated by Graham, Sabbata and Zook, not only illustrate the percentage of the population of each geographic area that has access to the internet but also how much access these geographic areas have (Graham & Sabbata & Zook 2015: 92). These numbers are often influenced by various factors such as population, government laws, the type of country (developed, developing or less developed), etc. For example, according to the statistics revealed by the World Bank, China, at the time of writing, has a population of about 1.4 billion people. Statistically speaking, China has a much higher population in comparison to other developing countries like South Africa, which has a population of +/- 59 million at the time of writing. This will drastically affect the percentage of internet users as South Africa's population is only around 4.2% of China's entire population. Even though South Africa's population is significantly less, its internet penetration is within in same range (20%-40%). More developed countries like Canada, Germany, and Sweden have a lower population but also a higher internet penetration (Graham & Sabbata & Zook 2015: 92). This also shows how less developed and developing countries are isolated from the world as they cannot afford to use the internet or do not have the available infrastructure to access the internet.

Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results:

This reading explains how information is filtered according to the user's location. It also describes the importance of “digital augmentations” (Ballatore & Graham & Sen, 2017: 1195). The information certain countries have available is far less than more digitally powerful countries. With this, the information created in less developing and developing countries are less likely to reach more developed countries.

These two readings are related as they both discuss how less developed and developing countries are isolated from the digital world. Due to the expensive nature of the internet and how information is filtered according to one's location, it is easier for countries that have a higher internet penetration percentage in comparison to other countries.
